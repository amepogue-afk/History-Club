Why Sacramento State

A serious research university that still feels like home.

Sacramento State recently earned R2 Carnegie Research Classification — a designation that means this is a place where original scholarship happens, not just coursework. When you study here, you learn from faculty who are actively contributing to historical knowledge, not just teaching from someone else's textbook.

"Your professors have published books. They've been in the archives. They know what real historical research looks like — because they do it."

And unlike the large UC campuses down the road, you'll actually get to know them.

What You Actually Learn

The skills employers keep asking for.

When employers say they can't find people who can write, think critically, or communicate complex ideas clearly — they're describing exactly what a history degree produces. These aren't soft skills. They're the ones that differentiate people in almost every professional field.

Research & Investigation

Finding, evaluating, and synthesizing information from complex, contradictory, or incomplete sources.

Written Communication

Constructing clear, evidence-based arguments in writing — one of the most consistently in-demand professional skills.

Critical Analysis

Interrogating sources, identifying bias, distinguishing correlation from causation, and evaluating competing claims.

Cross-Cultural Fluency

Understanding how context, culture, and perspective shape events, decisions, and people — essential in any global environment.

Oral Presentation

Making complex arguments accessible and persuasive to a variety of audiences.

Long-Form Thinking

Managing large projects, sustained arguments, and complex material over time — the discipline that drives every career forward.

Real Careers, Real People

What history graduates actually do.

These are the kinds of careers a history degree opens — across industries, sectors, and fields you might not expect.

Government & Public Service

The Government Historian

"I get to travel the world conducting research. My history degree taught me to ask the right questions — and that's the whole job."

History graduates work as historians for federal agencies including the State Department, Department of Defense, and National Park Service. They conduct field research, manage archival collections, and write the official record of institutions that shape public life.

Archives & Special Collections

The Archivist

"I can't tell you how many times I've drawn on what I learned studying historiography. Those questions stay with you your whole career."

From university libraries to corporate archives, city governments to international institutions, archivists preserve and provide access to the records that make history possible. A history BA pairs naturally with a library science MA to open this path.

Law & Policy

The Attorney

"Law school was a direct extension of what I learned to do as a history major — research, write, argue from evidence. The transition was natural."

History is one of the strongest pre-law majors because it builds exactly the skills law schools select for. LSAT scores, writing ability, and comfort with complex reading materials all benefit from a rigorous history education.

Education

The Educator

"As teachers we're expected to use multiple resources, reading, and writing to help students make deeper connections. Those are precisely the things I learned in my history courses."

From high school classrooms to community colleges and universities, history educators play a critical role in shaping how students understand the world. California's schools need qualified history teachers — and the job market reflects it.
